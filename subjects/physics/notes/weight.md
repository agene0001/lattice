# Weight

**Intuition.** Weight is the *force* gravity exerts on a mass — not the mass itself. Your mass is the same everywhere; your weight changes with gravity (less on the Moon).

## Definition

Weight is a special case of Newton's second law where the acceleration is gravity, $g$:

$$W = mg,$$

with $g \approx 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$ near Earth's surface. Weight is a force, so its unit is the newton ($\text{N}$).

## Worked example

A $10\,\text{kg}$ object near Earth's surface:

$$W = mg = (10\,\text{kg})(9.8\,\text{m/s}^2) = 98\,\text{N}.$$

## Why it matters

Weight is the force you balance against in every statics and free-fall problem — the pull that a normal force, tension, or thrust must overcome. It's also why all objects fall with the same acceleration: heavier mass means more weight, but also more inertia, and the two cancel.

> **Common pitfall.** Mass ($\text{kg}$) and weight ($\text{N}$) are different quantities — a scale that reads "kg" is really inferring mass from weight assuming Earth's $g$. Never report a weight in kilograms.
