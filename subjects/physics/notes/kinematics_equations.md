# Kinematic Equations

**Intuition.** When acceleration is constant, four equations relate the five quantities of motion — displacement $s$, initial velocity $u$, final velocity $v$, acceleration $a$, and time $t$. Know any three, find the rest.

## The equations

$$v = u + at$$
$$s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$$
$$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

They all assume $a$ is constant over the interval.

## Worked example

An object is dropped from rest, so $u = 0$ and $a = g = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2$. How fast is it moving after $t = 2\,\text{s}$? Use the first equation:

$$v = u + at = 0 + (9.8\,\text{m/s}^2)(2\,\text{s}) = 19.6\,\text{m/s}.$$

## Why it matters

These are the workhorses of introductory mechanics — projectiles, braking distances, free fall — and they're just the integrals of constant acceleration. Each one is calculus in disguise.

> **Common pitfall.** They only hold for **constant** acceleration. For a changing force (a spring, air resistance), you can't use them — you integrate the actual $a(t)$ instead. Also mind signs: taking "up" as positive makes $g$ negative.
