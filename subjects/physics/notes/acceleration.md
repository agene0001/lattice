# Acceleration

**Intuition.** Acceleration is how quickly velocity changes — speeding up, slowing down, or turning. If velocity isn't changing, acceleration is zero, no matter how fast you're going.

## Definition

Average acceleration is the change in velocity over the time it took:

$$\bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v - u}{t},$$

where $u$ is the initial velocity and $v$ the final. Its unit is metres per second per second, $\text{m/s}^2$.

## Worked example

A runner speeds up from rest ($u = 0$) to $30\,\text{m/s}$ in $6\,\text{s}$:

$$a = \frac{30\,\text{m/s} - 0}{6\,\text{s}} = 5\,\text{m/s}^2.$$

## Why it matters

Acceleration links motion to *force*: Newton's second law says a net force produces an acceleration, $F = ma$. It's also the second derivative of position, $a = \dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2}$.

> **Common pitfall.** Constant *speed* around a curve still means you're accelerating — the *direction* of velocity is changing. Acceleration responds to any change in velocity, not just changes in magnitude.
