# Velocity

**Intuition.** Velocity is how fast position changes *and in which direction* — displacement per unit time.

## Definition

Average velocity is displacement $\Delta x$ divided by the time taken $\Delta t$:

$$\bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}.$$

Its SI unit is metres per second, $\text{m/s}$. **Speed** is the magnitude of velocity (no direction); velocity is a vector, so it also has a sign or direction.

## Worked example

A car travels $100\,\text{m}$ in $5\,\text{s}$:

$$\bar{v} = \frac{100\,\text{m}}{5\,\text{s}} = 20\,\text{m/s}.$$

## Why it matters

Velocity is the first derivative of position, $v = \dfrac{dx}{dt}$ — the bridge from kinematics to calculus. Everything in motion builds on it: acceleration is the rate of change *of* velocity.

> **Common pitfall.** Average velocity uses *displacement* (net change in position), not distance travelled. A runner who does a full lap back to the start has zero average velocity even though the distance run was large.
