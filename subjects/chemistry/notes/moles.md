# The Mole

**Intuition.** Atoms are far too small to count one by one, so chemists count them by *weighing*. A mole is just a fixed, enormous number of particles — the chemist's "dozen."

## Definition

One **mole** is $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ particles (Avogadro's number, $N_A$). It's a counting unit: "1 mole of water" means that many water molecules, just as "1 dozen eggs" means 12.

The number of moles is the count divided by $N_A$, or — far more usefully — the mass divided by the molar mass:

$$n = \frac{m}{M}.$$

## Worked example

How many moles are in $36\,\text{g}$ of water, given $M = 18\,\text{g/mol}$?

$$n = \frac{36\,\text{g}}{18\,\text{g/mol}} = 2\,\text{mol}.$$

The grams cancel with the grams in $\text{g/mol}$, leaving moles.

## Why it matters

The mole is the bridge between the lab (grams you can weigh) and the equation (particles that react in whole-number ratios). Every stoichiometry calculation passes through it.

> **Common pitfall.** Watch the units in $n = m/M$: mass in grams over molar mass in $\text{g/mol}$ gives moles, because the units divide out. If your answer comes out in grams, you divided the wrong way.
