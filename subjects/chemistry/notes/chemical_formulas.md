# Chemical Formulas

**Intuition.** A chemical formula is a recipe: it names the elements in a substance and, with subscripts, how many atoms of each are in one unit.

## Reading a formula

In $\ce{H2O}$, the subscript $2$ applies to the H immediately before it, so one molecule has **2 hydrogen atoms and 1 oxygen atom** (a missing subscript means 1). In $\ce{CO2}$: 1 carbon, 2 oxygen.

Parentheses group atoms, and a subscript outside multiplies everything inside: $\ce{Ca(OH)2}$ has 1 calcium, 2 oxygen, and 2 hydrogen.

## Worked example

Count the atoms in $\ce{H2SO4}$ (sulfuric acid):

- H: $2$
- S: $1$
- O: $4$

Six atoms in all, held together as one unit.

## Why it matters

Formulas are the alphabet of chemistry — every equation, every mass calculation, and every mole conversion starts by reading one correctly. Get the atom count wrong and the molar mass and balancing that follow are wrong too.

> **Common pitfall.** A subscript binds only to the symbol right before it, while a coefficient in front (as in $\ce{2 H2O}$) multiplies the *whole* formula. $\ce{2 H2O}$ is 4 H and 2 O — not the same as $\ce{H2O2}$.
