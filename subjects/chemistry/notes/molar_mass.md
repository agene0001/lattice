# Molar Mass

**Intuition.** The molar mass is how many grams one mole of a substance weighs. You get it by adding up the masses of all the atoms in the formula.

## Definition

Molar mass $M$ has units of grams per mole, $\text{g/mol}$. For a compound, sum the atomic masses (from the periodic table) of every atom in the formula, counting subscripts.

## Worked example

Molar mass of water, $\ce{H2O}$, using $\text{H} = 1$ and $\text{O} = 16$:

$$M = 2 \times (1) + 1 \times (16) = 18\,\text{g/mol}.$$

For $\ce{CO2}$ with $\text{C} = 12$, $\text{O} = 16$:

$$M = 12 + 2 \times 16 = 44\,\text{g/mol}.$$

## Why it matters

Molar mass is the conversion factor between grams and moles ($n = m/M$) — the single most-used calculation in chemistry. It's why reading a formula correctly matters so much: the atom count *is* the molar mass.

> **Common pitfall.** Multiply each atomic mass by its subscript before adding. For $\ce{CO2}$ the oxygen contributes $2 \times 16 = 32$, not $16$ — forgetting the subscript is the most common molar-mass error.
