# Balancing Equations

**Intuition.** Atoms are neither created nor destroyed in a reaction, so whatever goes in must come out — the same count of every element on both sides. Balancing is just bookkeeping that respects conservation of mass.

## The method

Put whole-number **coefficients** in front of formulas (never change subscripts — that would change the substance) until every element matches on both sides.

## Worked example

Balance $\ce{H2 + O2 -> H2O}$.

As written: left has 2 H and 2 O; right has 2 H and 1 O — oxygen doesn't match. Put a 2 in front of the water:

$$\ce{H2 + O2 -> 2 H2O}$$

Now O is balanced (2 and 2), but H is 2 on the left and 4 on the right. Fix hydrogen with a 2 in front of $\ce{H2}$:

$$\ce{2 H2 + O2 -> 2 H2O}$$

Check: H is $4 = 4$, O is $2 = 2$. Balanced, with coefficients $2, 1, 2$.

## Why it matters

A balanced equation is what makes stoichiometry possible — the coefficients are the ratios you use to convert between moles of reactants and products.

> **Common pitfall.** Balance by changing **coefficients**, never subscripts. Turning $\ce{H2O}$ into $\ce{H2O2}$ to balance oxygen is wrong — that's hydrogen peroxide, a different substance.
